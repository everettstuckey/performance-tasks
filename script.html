Presentation Script - SLPS Attendance Analysis

Slide 1

Title Slide

"Good afternoon, members of the Board. Today I'll be presenting our analysis of attendance trends across St. Louis Public Schools using DESE proportional-attendance data for 2023 through 2025 - with historical context going back to 2009."

Slide 2

Executive Summary

"Let me begin with the key takeaways:

  • SLPS proportional attendance rate - the share of students attending 90% or more of school days - stands at 46.3% in 2025
  • That is essentially unchanged from 2022 (also 46.3%) and well below our pre-pandemic peak of 88.3% in 2016
  • K-8 attendance (50.0%) remains roughly 13–14 points above high school (36.5%), but both are far below the state 90% target
  • Individual schools range from 9.4% (Vashon High) to 94.9% (Mallinckrodt ABI Elementary) - a spread of 85 percentage points
  • Compared with the STL metro area district average, SLPS lags by roughly 40 points

These numbers tell us chronic absenteeism is the defining challenge of this district - and that bright spots do exist."

Slide 3

District-Wide Attendance Trend (Chart)

"This chart shows SLPS's proportional attendance rate from 2009 to 2025.

Key observations over the last three years:

  • 2023: 49.4% - a +3.1 pp improvement over 2022's 46.3%
  • 2024: 49.9% - a modest +0.5 pp gain
  • 2025: 46.3% - a sharp −3.6 pp drop, erasing all post-pandemic recovery

Longer historical arc:

  • 2009–2016: Steady climb from 69.9% to a peak of 88.3%
  • 2017–2019: Gradual decline to 77.2%
  • 2020–2021: COVID-era collapse to 50.3% (2021)
  • 2022–2025: Stalled in the mid-to-upper 40s with no sustained recovery

Possible explanations for the 2025 decline:

  1. Pandemic-era habit persistence - Remote-learning routines normalized absence
  2. Economic pressures - Families facing housing instability, food insecurity, or needing students to work
  3. Transportation barriers - Bus route changes and limited public transit
  4. Health and mental-health challenges - Ongoing illness, trauma, and under-resourced school counseling
  5. School climate - Safety concerns and student disengagement

The green dashed line at 90% represents the state target - we have a 44-point gap to close."

Key takeaways:

  • K-8 consistently outperforms high school by 13–15 points

Why the grade-level gap?

  • Younger students depend on parents for transportation and daily routine
  • Adolescents have more autonomy and face competing pressures - work, childcare, disengagement
  • High-school coursework may feel disconnected from students' goals
  • Social dynamics and peer influence intensify with age

Slide 5

School-Level Performance (2025)

"This slide shows our highest- and lowest-performing buildings in 2025.

Top Performers:

  • Mallinckrodt ABI Elementary: 94.9%
  • Metro High: 91.7%
  • Betty Wheeler Classical Jr. Academy: 89.4%
  • Collegiate School of Medicine/Bioscience: 87.8%

These four schools are meeting or approaching the 90% target. What are they doing differently?

Lowest Performers:

  • Vashon High: 9.4% - roughly 1 in 10 students attending regularly
  • Yeatman-Liddell Middle: 16.1%
  • Long Middle: 16.9%
  • Sumner High: 17.5%

Possible factors differentiating high vs. low performers:

  • Magnet/specialty programs may attract more engaged families
  • Geographic location and neighborhood safety
  • School culture, relationship-building, and leadership stability
  • Family engagement strategies and wraparound services
  • Smaller, mission-driven schools may foster stronger community

Slide 6

Schools with Largest Changes (2023 → 2025)

"This chart highlights schools that experienced the most dramatic swings over the three-year window.

Most Improved:

  • Carnahan Middle: +28.4 pp (35.7% → 64.1%)
  • Miller Career Academy: +10.4 pp (22.7% → 33.1%)
  • Academy of Entrepreneurial Study: +8.8 pp (30.3% → 39.1%)
  • Metro High: +7.1 pp (84.6% → 91.7%)

These schools found something that works. We should study and replicate their approaches.

Most Declined:

  • Nahed Chapman New American Academy: −27.4 pp (54.6% → 27.2%)
  • Bryan Hill Elementary: −26.3 pp (74.0% → 47.7%)
  • Hickey Elementary: −24.2 pp (64.5% → 40.3%)
  • Long Middle: −24.1 pp (41.0% → 16.9%)

Possible explanations for dramatic declines:

  • Leadership or staffing turnover
  • Demographic shifts in the school community
  • Loss of key programs or resources
  • Neighborhood instability affecting safety or housing
  • For Nahed Chapman specifically - this school serves immigrant/refugee families who may face unique barriers

These declines warrant immediate investigation and targeted intervention."

Slide 8

Key Findings & Recommendations

"To summarize:

  1. Chronic Absenteeism Crisis - With a district-wide rate of 46.3%, more than half of students are chronically absent. This has cascading effects on achievement, graduation, and life outcomes.
  2. No Sustained Recovery - Despite a brief uptick in 2023-2024, 2025 attendance fell back to 2022 levels. The pandemic's impact has become structural.
  3. Grade Level Gap - The 13–15 pp gap between K-8 and high school means we are losing students as they age. Middle school is the critical inflection point.
  4. Bright Spots Exist - Mallinckrodt, Metro High, Classical Academy, and Collegiate Med/Bio prove high attendance is achievable. These models must be studied and scaled.
  5. Urgent Intervention Needed - Several schools have rates below 20%. These require immediate, intensive, school-specific plans.
  6. Equity Gaps - Racial and socioeconomic disparities in attendance are severe and worsening.

I recommend we form an Attendance Task Force, conduct site visits to top performers, and develop a district-wide attendance improvement plan with measurable targets.

Thank you. I'm happy to take questions."

Appendix

Additional Talking Points

If asked about COVID-19 impact:

"SLPS peaked at 88.3% in 2016 - well before COVID. The pandemic accelerated an existing decline but the post-pandemic stall at ~46-50% suggests the causes are now structural: economic hardship, housing instability, transportation, and eroded school-engagement norms."

If asked about comparison to other districts:

"The STL MSA suburban district average remains in the mid-80s, roughly 40 points above SLPS. Kansas City Public Schools (KCPS) faces a similar crisis, hovering in the low-to-mid 50s. STL-area charter schools average in the mid-60s - better than SLPS but still below the state target. The dashboard allows direct comparison across all these groups."

If asked about resources needed:

"Effective attendance interventions typically require:

  • Dedicated attendance specialists and social workers at every building
  • Early warning systems to flag at-risk students within the first 10 days
  • Family outreach coordinators - home visits, not just phone calls
  • Transportation solutions - especially for high-school students
  • Incentive programs tied to attendance milestones
  • Mental health support services embedded in schools"

If asked about accountability:

"Attendance is a shared responsibility across families, schools, and the district. We need school-level improvement plans with quarterly benchmarks, principal accountability for attendance outcomes, and community partnerships - but always paired with adequate resources. Punitive approaches alone do not work."

If asked about the data source:

"All figures come from the Missouri Department of Elementary and Secondary Education (DESE) Proportional Attendance dataset. The metric measures the percentage of students who attended 90% or more of enrolled school days. The dashboard is available for interactive exploration."
